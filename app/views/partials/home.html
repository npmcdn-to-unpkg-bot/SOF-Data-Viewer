The Sound of Fractions

The Sound of Fractions (SoF) is a new way to approach teaching fractions to students. We use music and rhythm to capture students' interest and challenge mathematical questions to them that they can answer musically using rhythms.

SoF utilizes several key components to help students begin to understand the system of fractions:

Music
  • is embodied
  • can be entrained
  • is fun to perform
  • is easy to enjoy
Math (particularly fractions)
  • is ordered
  • is scalar
  • is abstract and concrete

Some important challenges

When we use music to interact with numbers and fractions we can ask important challenging questions like:
  • Which comes first? A the second beat in a rhythm with 3 beats or the second beat in a rhythm with 4 beats?
  • Who plays more? Someone who plays one half of a rhythm with 6 beats, or someone who plays one third of a rhythm with 10 beats?
  • What is similar and what is different when you compare a rhythm that has 6 beats and lasts half as long as a rhythm that that has 3 beats.

The most challenging problems lead to the most promising results

How can a micro-world that utilizes additional representations provide meaningful resources for learning, and address misconceptions that are blocking student learning?
Can students who struggle with fractions make meaningful progress in their composition, comprehension, and communication when approaching the system of fractions from perspectives related to music?
How do multiple, novel, rich, and interactive culturally appropriate representations affect engagement in a learning environment?
We help students who struggle with the problem of fractions by approaching it from a different vantage point.

We start with Music ...

Creating Music
Students begin by listening to music they are familiar with, and then creating their own short rhythms.
Identifying Music
Students are then challenged to describe rhythms.
Manipulating Music
Students then alter rhythms matching them against mathematical descriptions.

... and move towards Math

Creating Math
Students adjust the rhythms using traditional math functions such as multiplication (play twice as long), division (play half as fast), addition (adding a beat to a rhythm), and subtraction (subtracting a beat to a rhythm).
Identifying Math
Finally, students must defend their descriptions with their peers using standard math terms and descriptions of their rhythms and adjustments.
Manipulating Math
Students use stronger knowledge of math terms to describe robust rhythms and apply their strategies to more traditional math.

Representations

Our models students can interact with

Students can choose to add representations to their instruments. Each representation is a different visual model of the same audio sound rhythm.
(All examples below have 5 beats, with the first, third, and fifth beats playing [selected and colored], and the second and fourth beats not selected [gray and half opacity])

Features of our Models

The Delta (∆) button allows students to transition the representation form one Core Four to another.


The Pile is where additional beats can be added to the main model or placed back here.
Each representation can be removed by clicking the 'X'.

Each beat can be toggled by clicking on them directly in the main model.

The bottom right can be grabbed to stretch or shrink the representation.

Audio

  • Blinks every time a beat that is turned on is played.
  • Blinks when a rhythm is being tapped so that student knows the computer heard the tap.
  • Helps the student align the mental and entrained version with the one they hear and see.
  • The record button in the lower left allows students to record their own rhythms.

Playback Animations

It is important to teach students that system of fractions helps describe real things in many different ways, some ways of describing are better than others, and that they can have similarities and differences that are important. To help with this, we animate all beats at the same time the underlying accompanied beat plays as seen below.

Transitions

Students can choose to transition any of the Core Four representations to another. Since the Light representation is focused on the temporal idea, we do not support graphical transitions between the Core Four and the Light.
